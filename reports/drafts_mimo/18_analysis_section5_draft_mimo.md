## 5.2 Success Modes

Cultural-QLoRA excels when queries target implicit social knowledge rather than explicit facts. Its success is pronounced in scenarios demanding **pragmatic appropriateness**. For instance, questions about workplace etiquette, hierarchical communication, and familial roles elicit responses that feel natively Korean—using indirect language, emphasizing harmony, and referencing collective well-being. This is because the training data (CultureBank, Nemotron) is rich in such discourse, and the Hofstede prior strongly primes the model for these dimensions. The effect is particularly strong in longer, discursive answers where cultural norms heavily guide structure and diction.

## 5.3 Failure Modes

The model struggles in three primary domains. First, **highly factual or specialized knowledge** (e.g., KMMLU-style questions) sees a slight performance drop, suggesting the cultural focus may divert processing resources from pure factual recall. Second, in **ambiguous bias benchmarks** like KoBBQ, the cultural conditioning can lead to over-hedging or misapplied cultural assumptions, sometimes avoiding a literal correct answer in favor of a perceived culturally-neutral (but incorrect) one. Third, **cross-cultural transfer** is a notable failure; when the system prompt specifies a different cultural context (e.g., "Answer from a US perspective"), the model's strong Korean cultural prior often overrides, defaulting to a Korean viewpoint. This indicates the LoRA weights and system prompt are powerful but not fully controllable.

## 5.4 Mechanism Hypothesis

We propose a two-stage mechanism for cultural conditioning. Initially, the **Hofstede system prompt acts as a strong prior**, setting a high-level behavioral guidepost (e.g., "prioritize group harmony"). This dominates short, direct responses. For longer generations, the **Cultural-KR LoRA adapter takes over**, guiding token-by-token generation to incorporate culturally specific vocabulary, sentence structures, and narrative patterns learned from the training corpus. This explains why ablation studies (Section 4.4) show that even a simple collectivist prompt (IDV-only) can significantly shift output, but the full six-dimensional conditioning produces the most holistic, consistent cultural alignment across diverse queries.

## 5.5 Anomaly: Cultural-KR Bias Increase

A key finding is that Cultural-KR exhibited a **higher bias score on KoBBQ** than the vanilla base model (0.487 vs. 0.450). This is not a failure of training but a revelation of its nature. The training corpora (CultureBank, Nemotron) are archives of human cultural discourse, which inevitably include embedded societal stereotypes and biases. By fine-tuning on this data to "sound Korean," the model did not just learn neutral cultural practices but also absorbed the implicit biases present in that discourse. Therefore, this increase is a legitimate and important finding: **cultural alignment is inseparable from cultural bias**. It underscores that aligning models to human cultures is an ethically complex task, not a purely technical one.

## 5.6 Hofstede Dimension Contribution

Analysis of the ablation study reveals that conditioning on **Individualism-Collectivism (IDV) alone** provides a large share of the observed behavioral shift, particularly for social and relational prompts. This is likely because IDV most directly governs the individual's relationship to the group, which is a fundamental underpinning of the Korean social scenarios in our test set. However, the **full six-dimensional configuration** consistently outperforms any single dimension, indicating that dimensions like Power Distance (PDI) and Long-Term Orientation (LTO) provide crucial, complementary nuance. For example, PDI is essential for correctly modeling hierarchical speech acts, while LTO informs views on family and persistence. The cultural signal is therefore composite, but IDV is the most consequential dimension for ordinary social interaction.
