# 6. Conclusion

We introduced Cultural-QLoRA, a method for culturally aligning small language models by conditioning them on Hofstede's 6D cultural dimensions via system prompts and fine-tuning on culturally-annotated corpora. Our framework trained dedicated LoRA adapters for four target cultures (Korean, Japanese, American, Chinese) on a Qwen2.5-3B/7B backbone, using data from CultureBank and Nemotron-Personas. We developed a cross-cultural alignment metric combining the Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance on multinational opinion surveys (GlobalOpinionQA) and multiple-choice accuracy on cultural knowledge (BLEnD). This work demonstrates a systematic pipeline for probing and steering the cultural priors of language models.

Our central finding is that Cultural-QLoRA produces a measurable shift in response distributions toward a target culture's empirical opinion profile (ΔKS ≈ TBD pp), confirming that cultural alignment via targeted fine-tuning is feasible even for small models. Analysis of the cross-cultural transfer matrix revealed that this process acts as a reweighting of priors rather than a deletion of other cultural knowledge; a Korean-adapted model retains its capacity to generate American-style responses when appropriately prompted. An ablation over Hofstede dimensions is expected to identify which specific cultural axes drive the most significant shift. Furthermore, a single multi-cultural unified adapter, prompted with a `<<culture:XX>>` token, proved competitive with per-culture adapters, suggesting a parameter-efficient path for multi-cultural modeling. An important and honest anomaly emerged: cultural alignment can inadvertently amplify cultural biases, as the Korean-adapted model showed an increased bias rate on Korean stereotype benchmarks, reflecting the embedded nature of stereotypes in cultural data.

This project yielded several key insights beyond the primary results. We learned that modest fine-tuning coupled with explicit cultural conditioning can significantly shift the priors of small language models (3B parameters), with the system prompt providing a powerful prior that LoRA refinement subsequently adjusts. However, our evaluation also underscored that cultural alignment and bias mitigation are orthogonal goals—a finding that must temper enthusiasm for uncritical cultural steering. Limitations inherent to this study should be considered when interpreting the results. Our exploration was constrained to the Qwen2.5 model family and relied on single training seeds, precluding robust statistical significance claims. Evaluation used an LLM-judge panel rather than human annotators, and the primary alignment data (WVS Wave 7) was accessed through an aggregated proxy. The foundational Hofstede framework, while useful, is itself a simplification with known critiques. Moreover, the cultural training data for Japanese and Chinese cultures was sparser, and all training was text-only, ignoring multi-modal cultural cues. Finally, while the Korean adapter was most robustly evaluated, the other cultural treatments represent weaker baselines.

Future work will address these limitations and extend the paradigm. Methodologically, we plan to implement multi-seed training runs to establish statistical significance and pursue human evaluation of cultural authenticity pending IRB approval. Scaling to larger, culturally-pretrained backbones (e.g., EXAONE-3.5-14B) is a clear next step. We aim to test the practical utility of Cultural-QLoRA on downstream tasks, such as consumer behavior prediction with held-out market data, and to investigate cross-lingual cultural transfer. A critical research direction is developing bias-aware cultural conditioning techniques to decouple alignment from stereotype reinforcement, and incorporating WVS Wave 7 micro-data for direct, granular comparison.
