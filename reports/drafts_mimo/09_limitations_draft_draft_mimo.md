# 6. Limitations and Future Work

## Model and Data Scope
Our study's findings are constrained by the models and data we employed. We conducted experiments exclusively within the Qwen2.5 model family (3B and 7B parameters). This limits our ability to disentangle architectural effects from our cultural-QLoRA methodology. Furthermore, a notable confound exists between our model scales: the 3B model was fine-tuned from the original HuggingFace weights, while the 7B run utilized a pre-quantized 4-bit version from the Unsloth repository. This difference in the quantization source could influence results, though we do not expect it to alter the core cultural conditioning effect. The training data also presents a significant imbalance. The Korean (KR) dataset is substantially richer and more detailed (4,800 examples from Nemotron-Personas-Korea) compared to the CultureBank-derived datasets for Japanese (JP), Chinese (CN), and US English (US), which contain only 866, 690, and 3,420 examples, respectively. This imbalance means the cultural-QLoRA effect is most robust and interpretable for Korean, while results for other cultures are likely noisier and more sensitive to data idiosyncrasies. Future work should validate the method across diverse model families (e.g., Llama, Mistral, and Korean-pretrained models like EXAONE) and pursue more balanced, high-quality cultural persona datasets for all target locales.

## Evaluation
Our evaluation methodology, while informative, has several limitations that warrant caution in interpreting the results. All experimental runs used a single random seed, and we did not compute error bars for the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) statistics. This leaves the statistical stability of the reported distribution shifts uncertain. The cultural "authenticity" of model outputs was assessed using an LLM-judge panel (GPT-5.5, Claude-opus-4-7, Mimo-v2.5-pro) without human evaluation. While cost-effective, this approach risks propagating the biases of the judge models themselves. Although we excluded the KoBBQ benchmark from training data to avoid direct leakage, we did not formally measure potential memorization of the BLEnD test set via overlapping concepts in the CultureBank training data. Finally, the cross-cultural KS statistics are computed on very small sample sizes per question (n=6-8), making the estimated opinion distributions inherently noisy. Future work should incorporate multiple seeds with error bars, supplement LLM-judges with human raters, and systematically measure training-test data overlap to strengthen claims of cultural generalization.

## Cultural Construct Validity
We acknowledge the foundational critique that Hofstede's 6-D model, a framework derived from 1980s IBM employee surveys, is a reductive and potentially biased lens for capturing the complexity of national cultures. We use it not as ground truth for culture, but as a structured signal for initializing cultural priors. A related limitation is that our training data and evaluation frame "culture" in a monolithic, nation-state-aligned manner (e.g., "Korean" responses). This inevitably masks significant within-culture diversity along axes of generation, region, socioeconomic status, and gender. Consequently, the model may learn a dominant, stereotyped cultural profile rather than a nuanced representation. Furthermore, all training data is purely text-based, omitting the vast space of visual, auditory, and non-verbal cultural cues. Future research could explore more granular and dynamic cultural frameworks, explicitly model intra-cultural diversity, and investigate multimodal approaches to cultural representation.

## Downstream Applicability
The downstream consumer prediction demo presented is a proof-of-concept, not a validated application. It relies on 10 hand-picked, hypothetical shopping scenarios without ground-truth purchase data, preventing any quantification of real-world predictive accuracy. We have not conducted any deployment-quality testing, such as integrating the model into a production user-facing chatbot to measure latency, failure modes, or user perception. Therefore, the practical utility of cultural conditioning for commercial applications remains speculative. Future work should partner with industry to test the methodology against real business metrics and user feedback in controlled pilot deployments.

## Reproducibility Notes
We note significant practical challenges in reproducing this work. The training process on AWS was subject to multiple session interruptions, including instance restarts and disk space shortages. Consequently, final results were aggregated from multiple sessions, and some intermediate adapter checkpoints (e.g., for the Japanese Run-G) were lost and had to be retrained. Furthermore, we relied on the HuggingFace `datasets` library to fetch benchmark data, but did not pin specific dataset versions at the time of the experiments, which could affect future exact reproducibility. We have made all final model adapters and our processed training data available to mitigate these issues, and we encourage future researchers to use explicit dataset versioning and checkpoint management practices.
