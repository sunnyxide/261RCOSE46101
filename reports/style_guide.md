# Style guide (for Writer + human reviewers)

## Voice

- Direct, precise, evidence-led. No marketing tone.
- Active voice preferred over passive ("We measured X" > "X was measured").
- First-person plural ("we") for methodological choices.
- No hedge words beyond what data supports. Forbidden: "arguably",
  "perhaps", "might be considered". Allowed when honest: "we believe",
  "this suggests", "preliminary evidence".

## Citations

- Every numerical claim ends with a file reference: `[results/x/y.csv]`.
- Every paper citation uses BibTeX key: `[@author2024title]`.
- Never cite a paper that isn't in `bibliography.bib`. If needed, mark
  `[TODO citation: <topic>]` and the Librarian will queue a lit search.

## Formatting

- Sentence case for section headers.
- US English spelling (academic NLP convention; check with course staff).
- Single space after periods.
- Numerals for 10+, words for 0–9 except in tables.
- Korean terms appear in roman script with English gloss in parentheses
  on first use: "단톡방 (group chat)".

## Diagrams and tables

- Every table has a one-line caption stating what it shows.
- Every figure caption is self-contained (could be understood without
  the surrounding text).
- Color usage must work in grayscale print.

## Section length budgets

- Abstract: ≤ 250 words.
- Introduction: ≤ 800 words.
- Related work: ≤ 1000 words.
- Background: ≤ 600 words.
- Methods: ≤ 1500 words (human-authored).
- Results: ≤ 1200 words, tables count separately.
- Discussion: ≤ 800 words.
- Conclusion: ≤ 300 words.
- Total target: 8–10 pages ACL-style.

## What never appears

- "Importantly", "Interestingly", "Notably" (low information).
- Marketing language: "powerful", "innovative", "cutting-edge".
- Vague impact claims without numerical support.
- Lists where prose would do.
