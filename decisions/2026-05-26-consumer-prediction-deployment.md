# Real consumer behavior prediction — what we can defensibly claim

> Source: PPT promised "actionable persona generation" + ORBT integration
> framing in HANDOFF.md §6. This document distinguishes what level of
> consumer-prediction claim our paper can sustain versus what would need
> additional validation.

## 1. The four-tier claim ladder

Consumer-behavior prediction is a strong claim. Distinguishing tiers
prevents reviewer pushback.

### Tier 1 — *Persona authenticity* (what we'll deliver)
"Our pipeline generates Korean consumer personas that native speakers
rate as more culturally authentic than vanilla LLM output, on CURE
5-dimension Likert."

- Evidence: CAS measurements (W5)
- Risk: low — straightforward A/B test
- ORBT use: feed personas into existing demand-forecasting systems as
  input, replacing demographic stubs

### Tier 2 — *Group dynamics directional fidelity* (likely deliverable, scoped down)
"Personas placed in OASIS-simulated D2C scenarios exhibit conformity /
authority-adoption / consensus dynamics that DIFFER between vanilla and
culturally-grounded conditions, in directions consistent with Korean
cultural theory."

- Evidence: BAS directional reporting (R4 Tier-3 decision)
- Risk: medium — OASIS herd-behavior bias (R3) and ground-truth
  absence (R4) limit absolute claims
- ORBT use: scenario simulator for "what would happen in a launch?"
  with caveat that absolute numbers need calibration

### Tier 3 — *Quantitative consumer-response prediction* (NOT in W8 scope)
"Given a brand X campaign and target cohort, our pipeline predicts the
RATE of conversion / engagement / complaint."

- Evidence required: real consumer A/B test on a Korean D2C brand with
  side-by-side comparison
- Risk: high — would need 60+ day campaign cycle, partner brand, IRB
- ORBT use: this is the eventual product positioning, NOT the paper claim
- Future work: HANDOFF.md ORBT integration plan W8+

### Tier 4 — *Causal interventional prediction* (research-paper future work)
"If campaign X is run on cohort Y, response will be Z with confidence W%."

- Evidence required: prospective study with intervention + control
- Risk: extreme — methodological challenges of social experimentation
- Acknowledge as direction beyond paper scope

## 5. Three concrete things the paper CAN show that are *novel* for consumer prediction

### 5.1 Directional consistency with Hofstede-known dimensions

If we measure CCR/AAS/GCS deltas and Vanilla→Full-stack moves these
metrics in directions that ALIGN with Korea's Hofstede scores (high
collectivism IDV=18 → expect higher conformity; long-term orientation
LTO=100 → expect slower group consensus on novelty), that's a
**testable cultural-theory prediction confirmation**.

Reviewer-friendly framing:
> "Cultural grounding shifts persona behavior in directions predicted by
> Hofstede's collectivism dimension. This provides evidence that the
> grounding is operating on the theory-relevant axes, not just surface."

This is a real research contribution that DOESN'T require Tier 3
prediction validation.

### 5.2 Cross-condition response divergence on D2C scenarios

If we run a "neutral D2C campaign" through 6 conditions and observe
that conditions DIFFER in the AAS metric (e.g., full-stack shows
stronger response to celebrity endorsement than vanilla), that's
useful for **ORBT's "preview a campaign before running it"**
product use case.

Even without absolute calibration, the DIFFERENTIAL prediction is
deployable: "Brand X, your campaign drives 1.4× authority adoption
relative to Brand Y's last campaign."

### 5.3 Disagreement between persona conditions as red-flag detector

If Vanilla and Full-stack produce divergent responses on the same
scenario, that's a signal "this campaign has cultural risk".

Use case: ORBT runs both, flags any campaign where Δ > threshold.
Doesn't predict outcome but flags need for human review.
