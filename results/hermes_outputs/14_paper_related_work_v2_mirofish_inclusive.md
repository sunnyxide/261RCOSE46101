# Related Work

## 2.1 Measurement of cultural bias in LLMs

A growing body of work quantifies the gap between LLM performance and human cultural knowledge. CulturalBench evaluates 1,642 Chinese cultural questions across 21 LLMs; the best-performing system achieves 61.5% accuracy compared to 92.4% for human annotators, revealing that frontier models retain substantial cultural blind spots even at scale [chiu2024culturalbench]. In the Korean context, KoBBQ provides 322 question–answer pairs drawn from Korean BBQ-bias categories, enabling measurement of both factual accuracy and bias propagation in question-answering [jin2024kobbq]. CLIcK extends this to broader Korean linguistic competence, testing 10 Korean cultural knowledge categories against machine-translated English equivalents [kim2024click], while HAE-RAE Bench targets Korean commonsense and factual knowledge across six task formats [haerae2023]. Together, these benchmarks establish that multilingual LLMs systematically underperform on non-English cultural knowledge. Critically, Tao et al. show that cross-model error patterns are highly correlated (ρ > 0.97 across five major LLM families), suggesting that the failure is not model-specific but reflects shared training data distributions [tao2024crossmodel]. Our W3 pilot replicates this: GPT-4o, Gemma-2, and KoAlpaca all show degraded KoBBQ performance, confirming the universality of the gap. Our Section 4 results demonstrate that neither general Korean SFT alone (QLoRA on KoAlpaca) nor cultural retrieval alone closes it, motivating the layered grounding approach developed in Sections 3 and 4.

**Position**: Prior work establishes that the gap exists and is persistent across model families. Our contribution is a systematic decomposition of *interventions* — demographic, retrieval, parametric — measured against these same Korean-specific benchmarks.

## 2.2 Cultural value alignment and multi-agent simulation engines

Cultural value alignment aims to make LLMs internalize culturally appropriate reasoning. CultureLLM fine-tunes a single model on items from the World Values Survey and Schwartz Value Survey, demonstrating improved cultural understanding across 1,012 evaluation items spanning 119 countries [li2024cultureLLM]. CAReDiO [TODO citation: CAReDiO] proposes retrieval-augmented cultural reasoning, grounding responses in culturally specific knowledge rather than parametric memory. CultureBank provides a community-driven cultural knowledge graph covering 14 distinct cultural groups, offering structured cultural descriptors that can serve as retrieval targets [shen2024culturebank]. These approaches share a common limitation: they evaluate through static benchmarks or heuristic scoring, not through simulated social interaction. The multi-agent simulation paradigm addresses this differently. OASIS implements a multi-agent social simulation platform on CAMEL-AI, enabling agents to interact through simulated social media, dating, and recruitment environments [yang2024oasis]. MiroFish builds directly on OASIS to create a general-purpose prediction engine with over 62,000 GitHub stars, implementing a five-stage workflow — Graph, Environment, Simulation, Report, Interaction — that uses Zep Cloud memory and Qwen-plus to generate trajectory predictions for politics, finance, and narrative outcomes [ji2026mirofish]. MiroFish-Offline [mirofishoffline2026] demonstrates that the Zep Cloud dependency is replaceable with Graphiti+Neo4j, confirming architectural portability. MiroFish evaluates predictions by report quality rather than standard cultural benchmarks, leaving the alignment and representativeness of its simulated populations unexamined.

**Position**: MiroFish and OASIS establish the multi-agent simulation substrate we build upon. Our contribution extends it with three cultural-grounding layers — Nemotron-quota demographics, CultureBank-Korean retrieval via LightRAG, and optional QLoRA parametric injection — and validates each layer against standardized Korean cultural benchmarks rather than heuristic evaluation.

## 2.3 Synthetic persona generation

Synthetic persona generation underpins any agent-based social simulation. PersonaHub [TODO citation: PersonaHub] generates diverse personas from seed descriptions, but targets English-dominant populations without cultural grounding beyond surface demographics. Ge et al. scale persona generation to one billion synthetic individuals, demonstrating that diversity in age, location, and occupation can be achieved at industrial scale, though cultural value alignment remains unaddressed [ge2024scaling]. Nemotron-Personas-Korea provides a structured Korean demographic skeleton calibrated against KOSIS census distributions across 14 demographic dimensions, ensuring statistical representativeness of the Korean population [nvidia2024nemotron]. This skeleton is the foundation of our W3 data backbone. However, prior persona work shares a structural limitation: demographic surface features (age, region, occupation) and linguistic surface (Korean-language prompts) are treated as sufficient. The result is personas that are linguistically Korean but culturally thin — "Americans named Min-jun" — lacking the specific cultural references, values, and reasoning patterns that distinguish genuine Korean cultural cognition. Our approach layers CultureBank-Korean cultural descriptors onto Nemotron-demographic skeletons through LightRAG retrieval, grounding each synthetic agent in both census-distributed demographics and culturally specific knowledge. Section 4 demonstrates that this combination produces measurable improvement on KoBBQ bias metrics compared to either layer alone.

**Position**: Prior persona generation work achieves demographic and linguistic fidelity but lacks cultural depth. Our contribution integrates structured demographic quotas with community-sourced cultural knowledge retrieval, producing Korean personas grounded in both census data and cultural practice.

## 2.4 Korean-pretrained LLMs as alternative baseline

A natural objection to our approach is whether cultural grounding is necessary at all if one simply selects a Korean-pretrained base model. Several strong Korean-capable LLMs exist. EXAONE 3.0 from LG AI Research is a 7.8B-parameter bilingual (Korean–English) model that achieves competitive Korean benchmarks [exaone2024]. HyperCLOVA X from Naver Cloud serves as the dominant Korean commercial LLM, trained on large-scale Korean web corpora [TODO citation: HyperCLOVA X]. KULLM v2 from Korea University's NLP/AI Lab provides an open-source Korean-specialized alternative [kullm2023]. Polyglot-Ko from EleutherAI offers multilingual architectures with Korean-specific training [TODO citation: Polyglot-Ko]. One might expect these models to outperform Western multilingual models on Korean cultural benchmarks simply through pretraining exposure. We directly test this hypothesis: our Run-E applies EXAONE 3.0 as the base model with identical QLoRA fine-tuning on KoAlpaca. We expect Korean-pretrained bases to outperform Western multilingual models on KMMLU (factual recall) but not necessarily on KoBBQ (bias propagation), because pretraining on Korean text does not inherently encode correct value judgments. We propose that cultural grounding — whether through Nemotron demography, CultureBank retrieval, or QLoRA cultural-instruction tuning — is a complementary and dominant intervention regardless of base model origin. Section 4 presents Run-E results alongside GPT-4o and Gemma-2 variants to test this claim directly.

**Position**: Korean-pretrained LLMs offer factual recall advantages but do not resolve the cultural alignment gap by construction. Our grounding layers operate orthogonally to base model selection and, we hypothesize, provide additive benefit across both Western and Korean-pretrained bases.
