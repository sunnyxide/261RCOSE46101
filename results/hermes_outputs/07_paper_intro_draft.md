# Introduction

Large language models deployed in production systems exhibit systematic Western-default behaviors that degrade user experience in non-Western markets [chiu2024culturalbench]. When a Korean enterprise customer asks an LLM-powered assistant to draft a team introduction, the model frequently romanizes Korean family names in given-name-first order, defaults to English-language politeness conventions that produce hollow or performative compliments by Korean social standards, and conducts multi-step reasoning in English before translating to Korean—a pattern that introduces compounding cultural drift at each reasoning step. These failures are not isolated edge cases; they reflect a structural gap between the cultural knowledge encoded during pretraining and the cultural competence required for deployment in specific linguistic communities.

Recent benchmarks quantify this gap with increasing precision. CulturalBench evaluates 1,696 multiple-choice questions spanning six cultural dimensions across 45 world regions, finding that even frontier models score below 60% accuracy on Korean-specific items [chiu2024culturalbench]. KOBBQ probes occupational bias in Korean contexts, revealing that models trained predominantly on English data reproduce gender and age stereotypes that diverge from Korean social norms [jin2024kobbq]. [TODO citation: tao2024pnas — PNAS paper on cultural value alignment showing systematic Western bias in LLM outputs across 100+ countries] These measurement efforts establish that the problem is real and widespread, but they do not solve it.

Three prior research directions attempt cultural adaptation but fall short of producing deployable artifacts. First, **measurement-only** work benchmarks cultural knowledge without modifying model behavior; CulturalBench [chiu2024culturalbench] and KOBBQ [jin2024kobbq] exemplify this category. Second, **value alignment** approaches such as CultureLLM [li2024cultureLLM] and [TODO citation: CareDIO — culture-aware DPO/RLHF alignment pipeline] adjust model outputs along abstract value axes but do not ground responses in specific demographic contexts. Third, **persona injection** methods including [TODO citation: PersonaHub — synthetic persona generation for LLM personalization] attach demographic labels to model inputs without verified correspondence to real population distributions. None of these approaches produces a system that simultaneously reflects a specific population's actual demographic structure, retrieves culturally specific behavioral descriptors, and validates output quality against behavioral criteria rather than knowledge recall alone.

We present a pipeline for culturally grounded LLM adaptation that addresses each of these shortcomings. Our approach combines five components: (a) demographic grounding from KOSIS-matched Nemotron Personas for the Korean population, where persona attributes correspond to census-recorded distributions across age, gender, region, and occupation [kosis2024; nvidia2024nemotron]; (b) cultural descriptor retrieval from the Korean subset of CultureBank [shen2024culturebank] via LightRAG [guo2024lightrag], providing context-specific behavioral norms; (c) QLoRA fine-tuning [ge2024scaling] for parametric injection of cultural knowledge that persists across prompting contexts; (d) behavioral validation through the OASIS framework [yang2024oasis], which evaluates outputs against interaction-level criteria rather than static benchmarks; and (e) integration into ORBT's production surface for direct measurement of cultural adaptation quality on live Korean enterprise workflows.

We test four hypotheses. **H1** (demographic fidelity): models fine-tuned on KOSIS-matched personas produce outputs whose demographic assumptions align with Korean census distributions at a rate ≥ 20 percentage points above ungrounded baselines, measured by our demographic-alignment metric [results/demographic_alignment.json]. **H2** (cultural descriptor utility): augmenting persona training with CultureBank-sourced behavioral descriptors increases OASIS cultural-appropriateness scores by ≥ 0.15 on a 0–1 scale relative to persona-only fine-tuning [results/cultural_descriptor_ablation.json]. **H3** (parametric persistence): QLoRA-injected cultural knowledge survives context-switching at ≥ 85% retention across 50 diverse prompt families [results/persistence_eval.json]. **H4** (validation reliability): OASIS behavioral scores correlate with blinded human preference judgments at Pearson *r* ≥ 0.7, establishing OASIS as a scalable proxy for human cultural evaluation [results/oasis_human_correlation.json].

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the pipeline architecture and each component's design rationale. Section 3 presents the experimental setup, including the autonomous laboratory methodology that generated training data, hyperparameter configurations, and evaluation protocols without manual intervention—a contribution we view as significant in its own right, as it enables reproducible cultural NLP research at a scale impractical with human-in-the-loop workflows. Section 4 reports results across all four hypotheses. Section 5 discusses implications, limitations, and broader applications. The appendix provides ablation studies for each pipeline component and detailed documentation of the autonomous lab infrastructure.
